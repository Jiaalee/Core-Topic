A Day in
 the Life

“It was [50] years ago today . . . “ The Beatles released Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts
Club Band on May 26, 1967 in the UK and on June 2 in the US. One of the signature
tracks on the album was John Lennon's magisterial “A Day in the Life”. Musically
brilliant and structurally mind blowing, no one had heard anything like it before.
Actually, it's a wonder anyone heard it all because it presented a number of unique
recording challenges according to Geoff Emerick, the lead sound engineer on
Sgt. Pepper's.

With today's digital recording technology, every sound being recorded can reside on
its own track and can be manipulated at will as it's fed into the mix. This gives the
recording engineers a lot of flexibility in adding, subtracting, and modifying the
elements of a song.

“A Day in the Life”, like everything else on Sgt. Pepper's, was recorded on four-track
tape machines which means that flexibility was limited as all of the sounds in the
song had to be present on those four tracks. The orchestral crescendo that occurs
twice in the song and the extraordinary sustained piano chord at the end turned
recording “A Day in the Life”into an epic sound engineering task. Emerick recounts
how he did it in his book (coauthored with Howard Massey) Here, There and
Everywhere: My Life Recording the Music of the Beatles.

The recording process for “A Day in the Life” began with a fortuitous accident. The
limitations of four-track recording required that the instrumental backing track for
the song be recorded in one go from start to finish. Normally, the Beatles worked out
an entire song before they laid down the backing track. However, the rest of the
studio that they wanted to lay down the backing track immediately. This presented a
problem because there was a 24-bar hole in the middle of the song that they didn't
know how they were going to fill.

They decided to go ahead anyway, leave the 24 bars blank, and record something in
the hole on the tape later. Mal Evans, one of the Beatles' roadies, was tasked with
counting off the 24 bars during the recording so the musicians could concentrate on
what they were going to play when the gap ended. Evans set an alarm clock off at
the start of the 24th bar to alert the Beatles that it was almost time to resume
playing. The tape picked up the alarm and Emerick couldn't find a way to eliminate it
from the mix.

As luck would have it, the alarm clock didn't turn out to be a problem. Paul
McCartney had a song snippet that he thought might work with the theme of “A Day
in the Life”. The snippet was planned to follow the 24-bar gap. His segment begins
with the lines “Woke up/ Got out of bed/ Dragged a comb across my head”. Although
he wasn't thinking about it at the time, McCartney's “Woke up” lyric changed Mal
Evans' alarm clock from a sound engineering problem to what may have sounded like
an inspired overdub.

With the alarm clock issue solved, the band was left with the problem of what to do
with the 24-bar hole in the song. Lennon suggested something that started out small
and grew into something huge. McCartney liked that idea and suggested doing it
with a full orchestra. George Martin, Sgt. Pepper's producer, said there was no way
EMI, the company that owned the Abbey Road studio wheret the album was being
made, would pay for a full orchestra to record 24 bars of music. Ringo solved the
problem by suggesting they hire half an orchestra and have them play it twice.
